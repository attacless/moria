How It Works
Full transparency on what MORIA does, how it protects you, and what no party - including us - can see. Every claim on this page is verifiable in the open-source codebase.
01 - Overview
What is MORIA?

MORIA is an anonymous, end-to-end encrypted chat room that requires no accounts, no emails, no phone numbers, and no personal information of any kind. You enter a shared secret - a password - and anyone else who enters the same password lands in the same room.

There is no room list, no directory, no way to browse. If you do not know the secret, the room does not exist to you.

What makes MORIA different from other encrypted messengers?

Most encrypted messengers route your messages through a server the operator controls. Even if messages are encrypted, the operator can see who is talking to whom, when, and how often.

MORIA has no such server. After an initial handshake over the decentralized Nostr relay network, messages travel directly peer-to-peer over WebRTC - an encrypted direct connection between your device and the other person's device. No relay, no operator, no third party sits in the path of your conversation.

02 - Encryption
How does the encryption work?

When you enter your shared secret, three things happen entirely in your browser - nothing is sent to any server:

1. Room ID derivation. Your secret is processed through Argon2id - a memory-hard key derivation function - using a domain-separated salt to produce a room ID. This hash is used to locate your peer on the Nostr network. No server ever sees your actual secret.

2. Room key derivation. A second, independent Argon2id derivation (16 MiB memory, 3 iterations) produces a 256-bit symmetric key used for dead drop encryption. This key never leaves your browser.

3. Session key derivation. When a peer connects, your device generates an ephemeral X25519 keypair. You exchange public keys with your peer over the encrypted WebRTC channel. An ECDH operation followed by HKDF-SHA256 produces a unique symmetric session key for that peer. This key is different for every session and is destroyed when you disconnect.

Every live message is encrypted with ChaCha20-Poly1305 using the per-peer session key before it leaves your device.

What is message padding?

Before encryption, every message is padded to a fixed 8,192-byte block using a 2-byte length prefix followed by the message content and cryptographically random noise. This means a short message like "hi" produces the exact same size ciphertext as a message at the maximum length.

Without padding, an observer could guess message content based on ciphertext length. The random noise fill - not zeros - ensures there is no distinguishable pattern in the plaintext before encryption. Padding eliminates this side channel entirely.

What is timestamp obfuscation?

Timestamps included in messages are rounded to the nearest 60 seconds before encryption. This prevents timing correlation attacks where an observer could match message patterns across different channels by comparing exact timestamps.

What is decoy traffic?

MORIA automatically sends encrypted dummy messages to all connected peers at random intervals between 10 and 60 seconds. These decoy messages are indistinguishable from real messages - they are the same size, encrypted with the same session key, and transit the same WebRTC channel.

The receiving client silently discards them after decryption. Decoy traffic defeats traffic analysis - an observer monitoring the WebRTC connection cannot determine when real communication is happening versus when the channel is idle.

03 - What Each Party Sees
What does the Nostr relay network see?

Nostr relays are used for two purposes: WebRTC peer discovery (signaling) and dead drop storage. Here is exactly what they see:

Signaling (SDP)
Encrypted WebRTC session negotiation data only. Not readable without your session keys.
Dead drop content
ChaCha20-Poly1305 ciphertext. Unreadable without the room key.
Room ID tag
Two independent Argon2id-derived hashes of your password - one for peer discovery, one for message storage. Relays cannot link the two without knowing your shared secret.
Event timestamp
When the dead drop was published. Accurate to the second.
Your IP address
Visible to relay operators unless you use a VPN or Tor.
Message content
Never. All content is encrypted before leaving your device.
Your identity
Never. No accounts, no persistent identifiers.
What does your peer see?

Your peer sees your messages after decryption, your ephemeral session alias (a random 8-character hex code generated fresh each session), and your presence in the room. Your peer also sees your public IP address via WebRTC ICE candidates exchanged during connection setup - this is a fundamental property of WebRTC direct connections. A VPN will show your VPN exit IP instead of your real IP address. Tor Browser prevents IP disclosure entirely but may affect connection reliability.

What does Attacless see?

Nothing. MORIA has no server, no database, no logging infrastructure, and no analytics. The app is a static file served by Cloudflare Pages. We have no mechanism to identify you, read your messages, or track your sessions.

04 - Queued Messages
What is a queued message?

When you enter a room with no one else present, MORIA operates as a digital dead drop. In intelligence tradecraft, a dead drop is a method of passing information between two people without them ever needing to meet or be in the same place at the same time.

You enter the shared secret, leave an encrypted message, and disconnect. The message is published to decentralized Nostr relays - encrypted and unreadable by anyone, including relay operators - where it waits for up to 24 hours. Your contact enters the same secret whenever they are ready and picks up the message.

How long do queued messages last?

All queued messages carry a 24-hour expiration tag (NIP-40). Compliant Nostr relays automatically delete them after this window. Messages are also removed from your local session when you disconnect.

When you or your peer manually confirms receipt by clicking MARK READ or MARK ALL READ, a 5-minute self-destruct timer begins. When both parties are online simultaneously, all queued messages auto-confirm the moment the second person joins - no manual action required.

Can queued messages be deleted before they expire?

Yes, with caveats. When you click TERMINATE, MORIA publishes a NIP-09 deletion request (kind 5 event) to the relay network, signed with the original ephemeral keypair used to publish each message. This is the strongest possible deletion signal - the signature proves the deletion was authorized by the original publisher.

Caveat
NIP-09 compliance varies by relay. Relays that support it will delete immediately. Relays that do not will retain the event until the 24-hour NIP-40 expiration fires. TERMINATE deletion is best-effort. The 24-hour TTL is the guaranteed fallback. If the room secret is compromised, previously published queued messages on non-compliant relays remain readable by anyone with that secret until expiry.
05 - Message Lifetimes
How long do messages last?

MORIA uses a tiered self-destruct system:

Queued message
24 hours on relay. Timer starts on MARK READ or peer join.
Both peers online
5 minutes from send. Presence = confirmed receipt.
Peer disconnects
Timer extends to 6 hours from the moment of disconnect.
After TERMINATE
Immediate local wipe. NIP-09 relay deletion attempted.

There is no archive, no backup, no way to recover a deleted message. All message state is held in browser RAM only - closing the tab destroys everything with no server-side copy.

06 - Security Features
What is the panic key?

Triple-tapping ESC within one second instantly wipes your session. When triggered:

All crypto key material is zeroed in memory. Browser storage (sessionStorage, localStorage, cookies) is cleared. The entire document is replaced with a blank page. If the browser attempts to restore the page from cache (back button, bfcache), the wipe re-triggers automatically.

The panic key is active from the moment the page loads - it does not require you to be in a room.

Important
The panic key clears your local session immediately. Messages already received by your peer cannot be retroactively erased from their device. Use TERMINATE if you need to attempt removal from both sides.
What is the TERMINATE button?

TERMINATE does three things simultaneously:

1. Broadcasts an encrypted TERMINATE signal to all connected peers. Their clients immediately wipe all messages you sent from their screens in real time.

2. Publishes NIP-09 deletion requests for any queued messages you left on Nostr relays, signed with the original ephemeral keypairs.

3. Zeros all local key material and returns you to the entry screen.

Unlike the panic key, TERMINATE is a coordinated wipe across all parties and all storage layers.

What is the inactivity timeout?

If you are inactive for 4 minutes - no typing, no mouse movement, no scrolling - a warning dialog appears. If you do not respond within the following minute, MORIA automatically disconnects you and returns to the entry screen. All session state is cleared.

This protects your session if you walk away from your device and prevents messages from burning while no one is actively reading.

What is steganographic mode?

Pressing Shift five times rapidly transforms the MORIA interface into a document editor appearance. Messages appear as paragraphs in what looks like a text document. All encryption, burn timers, and messaging continue operating normally underneath.

This is useful if your screen is visible to others. Press Shift five times again to return to the normal interface.

Are there any cookies or trackers?

No. MORIA sets no cookies, uses no analytics, loads no third-party scripts, and makes no external requests except to the Nostr relay network for peer discovery and queued message storage. The Content Security Policy headers enforce this at the browser level. You can verify this in your browser's developer tools.

Can I copy or screenshot messages?

MORIA actively discourages capturing message content. Text selection is disabled in the chat area, right-click context menus are blocked, and common screenshot keyboard shortcuts are intercepted. The clipboard is automatically cleared 15 seconds after any copy event, and immediately when you switch away from the tab.

These are friction-based protections, not absolute guarantees. A determined person can always photograph their screen with another device. The goal is to make casual capture difficult and reinforce the expectation that conversations in MORIA are not meant to be saved.

07 - Choosing a Secret
How should I choose a shared secret?

Your shared secret is the single most important piece of your security. It is both the key to your room and the foundation of your encryption. If someone guesses it, they can read everything - including queued messages still on relays.

Choose something long, random, and unguessable. A strong secret is at least 5-6 random words or 20+ mixed characters. Avoid names, dates, common phrases, or anything that could be found on your social media.

Share your secret through a secure out-of-band channel. In person is best. A phone call is acceptable. Never send it over text, email, or any platform that logs messages. If you suspect a secret has been compromised, stop using it immediately and agree on a new one.

08 - Trust Model
What are the residual risks?

No tool eliminates all risk. Here is an honest accounting of what MORIA cannot protect against:

Compromised room secret
All past and future messages in that room become readable. Change the secret immediately.
Device compromise
Malware or physical access to your device can read messages in RAM before encryption. MORIA cannot protect against a compromised device.
IP visibility to relays
Your IP address is visible to Nostr relay operators unless you use a VPN or Tor Browser.
Non-NIP-09 relay retention
Queued messages may persist up to 24 hours on relays that do not honor deletion requests.
Peer-side capture
MORIA cannot prevent your peer from photographing their screen. TERMINATE removes messages from their view but not from a photo already taken.
WebRTC STUN exposure
STUN servers used for NAT traversal may log connection metadata. Use Tor Browser to eliminate this.
Can government agencies access my messages?

MORIA has no server to subpoena and no operator with access to your messages. Live messages exist only in browser RAM and are destroyed on disconnect. Queued messages are encrypted blobs on third-party Nostr relays - relay operators can be compelled to produce them, but without your shared secret the ciphertext is computationally opaque.

Even under a valid legal order served to every relay in the network simultaneously, the most an adversary could obtain is a collection of encrypted blobs and room ID hashes - none of which are useful without the shared secret that only the participants know.
